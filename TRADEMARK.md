# InterGenOS — Trademark Policy

InterGenOS is free software. The **code** is licensed under the GNU
General Public License v3.0 or later — you may run it, study it,
modify it, and redistribute it under the terms of that license.

The **name and brand** are a separate matter. This document explains
which marks the project considers its identity, what uses are
permitted without permission, what uses require permission, and how
to request it.

The policy exists to protect users — not to constrain contributors,
reviewers, or honest commentary. The clarifying question for any use
is *"could this confuse a user about who is responsible for what they
are receiving?"* If yes, you need our permission. If no, you do not.

## 3. Permitted uses (no permission required)

You **may** do all of the following without asking us:

- **Refer to the project by name** in articles, reviews, social
  media posts, podcasts, videos, conference talks, academic papers,
  forum discussions, and any other commentary, criticism, or
  educational material. Honest reference is fair use under
  trademark law and is welcome.

- **Link to the project's website, repository, mirror, or
  documentation** using the wordmark "InterGenOS" or the logo, at
  appropriate size and in unmodified form.

- **Display the logo or wordmark in screenshots** of an installed
  InterGenOS system, the boot menu, the live ISO, the installer,
  the desktop, or the InterGen assistant, when the screenshots
  depict the original InterGenOS as we ship it. This includes
  using such screenshots in blog posts, documentation, comparison
  reviews, tutorials, books, and merchandise of your own that
  discusses or teaches InterGenOS.

- **Distribute unmodified copies** of the InterGenOS ISO, qcow2,
  or installed-system image with the InterGenOS name and logo
  intact. This is the case the GPL contemplates and the case the
  project actively encourages. You do not need our permission to
  share what we shipped.

- **Build the project from source** following our build system, on
  your own hardware, and use the resulting system under the
  InterGenOS name for **personal, educational, internal, or
  evaluation use**, provided the source you built from is the
  unmodified InterGenOS source as published. The trademark concerns
  in §4 below apply only to *distributing* a modified build under
  the project's name.

- **Create artwork inspired by InterGenOS** — desktop wallpapers,
  community emblems, conference posters, sticker designs — that
  reference the project but are visually distinct from the
  canonical mark. We encourage community art.

- **Use a generic factual description** of compatibility, such as
  "compatible with InterGenOS", "tested on InterGenOS", "runs on
  InterGenOS", "for InterGenOS users", on third-party software,
  hardware, services, documentation, and merchandise — provided
  the description is truthful and does not imply endorsement,
  affiliation, or origin from the InterGenOS project.

## 4. Permission required

You **must obtain written permission** from InterGenJLU before
doing any of the following:

- **Distributing a modified copy of InterGenOS under the InterGenOS
  name.** If you fork the code, customize the package set, change
  the default desktop, add proprietary components, modify the
  installer, or otherwise produce a derivative distribution that
  is not the InterGenOS we shipped — and you want to distribute
  the result publicly (download links, ISO mirrors, USB sticks at
  a conference, preinstalled on hardware you sell, etc.) — **you
  must rename it**. The GPL gives you the freedom to modify and
  redistribute the code; the trademark policy reserves the name
  for the original.

  This is the same pattern Mozilla, Debian, GNOME, and many other
  free-software projects use. You may distribute a derivative
  distribution under any name *other than* InterGenOS / InterGen /
  Forge / pkm. You should remove the InterGenOS mark and theme
  identifiers from your derivative.

- **Using the InterGenOS name or logo in a product name, company
  name, service name, domain name, software title, or other
  identifier that could be confused for an official InterGenOS
  product or service.** Examples of names that require permission:
  "InterGenOS Plus", "InterGenOS Pro", "InterGenOS Enterprise",
  "InterGenOS for Cloud", "intergenosforum.com", "intergenos-cloud.io".

- **Using the InterGenOS logo or wordmark in promotional material
  for a third-party product, service, or event** in a way that
  implies endorsement, sponsorship, partnership, or affiliation.
  "Powered by InterGenOS" is a factual claim that requires
  truthfulness; "Endorsed by InterGenOS", "Official InterGenOS
  Partner", and similar require explicit written permission.

- **Selling merchandise (T-shirts, stickers, mugs, etc.) bearing
  the InterGenOS mark or logo at scale.** Small-batch personal or
  community use without explicit permission is acceptable
  (see §3); commercial merchandise operations and online stores
  selling InterGenOS-branded goods require permission. The
  project may grant blanket permission to specific community
  print-on-demand stores in the future; that will be announced if
  it happens.

- **Registering domain names containing "intergenos", "intergen",
  "forge-os", "pkm-os", or close variants** for any purpose other
  than legitimate non-commercial community discussion, when such
  registration could be mistaken for an official project domain.
  We may not enforce against good-faith community sites
  (`intergenoswiki.example.com`, `intergenos.reddit-style-board.com`)
  but reserve the right to recover domains that create user
  confusion or are used for malicious purposes.

- **Filing applications for trademark or other intellectual
  property registration on any of the marks listed in §1**, or on
  marks confusingly similar to them, in any jurisdiction.

## 5. Why this matters (the user-protection principle)

The Prime Directive of InterGenOS says:

> *InterGenOS exists to put the user in control of their own
> machine.*

A user who downloads an ISO with the InterGenOS name on it has a
right to expect it is the InterGenOS we built and signed — not a
fork that quietly added a backdoor, a vendor edition that bundled
proprietary surveillance, or a malicious actor's payload presented
under our name. Trademark protection is the legal mechanism that
makes that expectation enforceable. **Without it, the GPL's freedom
to redistribute becomes a vector for impersonation attacks.**

The project's "Holy Grail" security posture — assuming adversaries
have superhuman vulnerability-discovery capabilities — applies here
too. An adversary who can mass-produce convincing malicious "InterGenOS
Plus" releases gains a privileged channel to compromise users who
trust the InterGenOS name. The trademark policy denies that channel.

This policy is **not** designed to prevent legitimate forking,
honest commentary, or downstream commercial use of the underlying
free software. It is designed to keep the InterGenOS name
attached only to InterGenOS — so that users can rely on the name
the way they rely on the cryptographic signature on the release
index. Both are integrity boundaries.

## 6. How to request permission

For all uses requiring permission under §4, email:

```
trademark@intergenos.org
```

with subject line:

```
Trademark Permission Request — <short description>
```

Include:

1. Your name (or organization name) and contact information
2. A description of the intended use — what you want to call it,
   how the mark or name will appear, where it will be distributed,
   what audience it targets
3. The scope and duration of the use you are requesting
4. Whether the use is commercial or non-commercial
5. (For derivative distributions) what modifications you intend
   to ship and how end users will be informed of the differences
   from upstream InterGenOS

We will respond within 30 days with one of:

- **Granted** — with any conditions (attribution, scope, time
  limit, renewal terms)
- **Granted in principle, pending details** — with a request for
  more specificity
- **Denied** — with the reason, so you can adapt the proposal
- **Conditionally granted** — with specific modifications
  (e.g., "please rename to remove 'InterGen' but you may
  reference compatibility")

A grant is **non-exclusive, non-transferable, and revocable in case
of misuse**. We do not charge for permission. We do not require
royalty or share of revenue. Conditions, where attached, are
designed to preserve user trust in the name — not to extract value
from the licensee.

## 7. Enforcement posture

InterGenJLU intends to enforce this policy **proportionately**:

- **First, education.** If you are using the marks in a way that
  exceeds this policy in good faith, our first action is to
  contact you and explain what change we need. The vast majority
  of unauthorized uses are unintentional and resolved in this
  step.

- **Second, formal request.** If education does not resolve the
  use, we will send a formal request to discontinue or rename.

- **Third, legal remedies.** For uses that are clearly intentional,
  commercial, or harmful to users (impersonation, malware
  distribution, fraud), we will pursue available legal remedies
  promptly — including DMCA-style takedowns of distribution
  channels, domain transfer proceedings, and trademark
  infringement claims.

We will **never** use trademark enforcement to silence honest
criticism, reviews, or commentary. The §3 permitted uses are
durable; nothing in this policy is designed to chill them.

## 8. Forks: friendly guidance

If you are forking InterGenOS to make a derivative distribution,
welcome — this is exactly the freedom the GPL preserves. Here is
how to do it cleanly:

1. **Rename your distribution** to something that does not include
   "InterGenOS", "InterGen", "Forge", or "pkm" as identifiers. A
   name that says what your fork is *for* (specific hardware, a
   specific use case, a specific community) is usually better
   marketing than a name that piggybacks on ours.

2. **Replace the brand assets**. Substitute your own logo, color
   palette, and shell theme. The asset directories at
   `assets/intergen-mark/` and `assets/intergen-shell-theme/`
   are the relevant locations.

3. **Update the package identifiers**. The `intergenos-default-settings`
   package (the gschema SSoT canonized by Owner Directive D-006)
   sets defaults that include the InterGenOS name. Rename or
   replace that package in your fork.

4. **Adjust the boot identifiers**. `/etc/os-release`, the GRUB
   boot menu, the live ISO label, and any visible filesystem labels
   should reflect your distribution's identity.

5. **Be honest about heritage**. You can — and should — describe
   your fork as "based on InterGenOS" or "derived from InterGenOS"
   in your documentation. That is accurate, permitted under §3,
   and helps users understand the lineage. *"Based on"* and
   *"derived from"* are factual statements; *"is"* and *"endorsed
   by"* and *"official"* are not.

6. **Provide your own source-availability mechanism**. The
   `SOURCES.md` commitment is the InterGenOS project's; your fork
   needs to make its own equivalent commitment for any code you
   distribute, including the InterGenOS-derived parts.

7. **Provide your own trademark policy** if you want to protect
   your fork's name.

We are happy to advise forks on naming choices that avoid
confusion. Email `trademark@intergenos.org` if you would like
informal guidance before you launch.
